# Menu principles

The site is opinionated. It cooks for one specific person, so the editorial rules are tight.

## 1. The eater

An 18/19-year-old guy. Works full-time. Trains most days. Wants:

- to feel full,
- to hit his protein,
- to enjoy the food,
- *not* to look at a plate of vegetables.

He is not the cook. The cook reads the recipe; the eater reads the plate.

## 2. Hide the vegetables

The non-negotiable. Vegetables are present in every dish for nutrition and bulk, but they're not visible or texturally obvious. Tactics:

| Technique | Works for | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Blend into a sauce** | tomato sauces, curries, stir-fry sauces | carrot, courgette, pepper, onion, spinach |
| **Grate into mince** | bolognese, chilli, cottage pie, meatballs | carrot, courgette, mushroom |
| **Purée under a topping** | cottage pie, traybakes | swede, cauliflower, sweet potato |
| **Mix into rice** | egg-fried rice, pilaf, burrito bowls | finely diced/grated carrot, peas mixed with sweetcorn |
| **Cook down to a jam** | pasta toppings, sandwich fillings | onion, pepper, tomato |

What we **don't** do: side salad, steamed broccoli on the plate, "just push them to the side" vegetables. If a vegetable shows up as a recognisable shape on the plate, the recipe has failed its brief.

We **do** call out which vegetables are hidden in each recipe — the cook needs to know what to buy and why. The recipe card surfaces a `Hidden veg` line. The eater never has to read it.

## 3. Protein first

Each dish targets **35–50 g of protein per serving** as its design centre. Not a hard floor, not a hard ceiling — a centre of gravity. Cheap, normal, supermarket protein:

- Chicken breast & thigh
- Lean beef mince (5–10% fat)
- Steak (rump / bavette for value)
- Eggs
- Tinned tuna, salmon fillets
- Greek yogurt as a sauce/marinade base
- Beans & lentils as a protein-extending bulk in mince dishes

Protein is shown numerically on every recipe card — the eater is tracking it.

## 4. Microwave-safe lunches

Lunches are reheated at work in a communal microwave. Constraints:

- **No textures that die in a microwave.** Crispy chicken katsu — beautiful at home, sad at a desk — is a *dinner* dish on this site, not a lunch.
- **No assembly required.** Single container, lid on, two minutes, eat. Lunches that need a fresh ingredient added at the office (e.g. "stir in the yogurt now") are a hassle and we avoid them.
- **Sauce-forward.** Saucy dishes (chilli, pasta, curry, rice bowls) reheat well because the sauce keeps the protein moist.
- **Travels in the bag.** No leaks. The recipe defaults to a sealed container, not a bento.

If a dish is great but doesn't survive the microwave, we put it on the dinner side of the week and pick something else for lunch.

## 5. Mon–Fri only

Five lunches and five dinners. Ten dishes a week. Weekends are deliberately excluded so the menu stays a tool for the working week, not a lifestyle commitment.

If the eater wants to eat any of these on a Saturday, fine — the site doesn't care. But the menu page only shows Mon–Fri.

## 6. Reuse over novelty

Variety is a nice-to-have, not the goal. If three lunches a week are dishes the eater already likes, that's a *win* — not a problem to solve. Rotation matters more than reinvention. New recipes earn their place by either:

- replacing something the eater is bored of, or
- filling a gap (we don't have a great Thursday dinner option, etc.).

## 7. Cooking-skill ceiling

The cook is competent but not a chef. Recipes assume:

- a normal hob, oven, microwave;
- a blender or stick blender (essential — most hidden-veg sauces start here);
- a sharp knife and one decent pan;
- 30–45 minutes on a weeknight, max.

No sous vide, no overnight brining, no specialist kit. If a technique would feel out of place in a normal home kitchen on a Tuesday, it doesn't go in.

## 8. What we won't do

- "Healthy" dishes that aren't satisfying.
- Calorie-counted diet meals. Protein matters; total-calorie obsessing doesn't.
- Visible-vegetable dishes dressed up as kid-food (e.g. "fun" broccoli trees). The eater is 18, not 8 — and the rule is *hide*, not *disguise*.
- Recipes that depend on a specific brand, niche supermarket, or specialty shop.
